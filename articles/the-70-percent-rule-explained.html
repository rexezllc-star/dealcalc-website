The 70% Rule Explained

Learn how investors use the 70% Rule to calculate maximum allowable offer, estimate repairs, and protect profit margins.

What is the 70% Rule in real estate?

The 70% Rule is a quick screening formula used by house flippers and wholesalers to estimate the maximum price they can pay for a property while leaving room for repairs, closing costs, holding costs, resale expenses, and profit. It is most commonly used for fix-and-flip deals where the investor buys below market, completes renovations, and sells near after-repair value.

The basic idea is simple: an investor should generally avoid paying more than 70% of the after-repair value minus estimated repair costs. This creates a margin of safety before deeper diligence begins.

The 70% Rule formula

Formula:
Maximum Allowable Offer = (After-Repair Value × 70%) − Estimated Repairs

For example, if a property has an estimated ARV of $300,000 and needs $45,000 in repairs, the formula would be:

  • $300,000 × 70% = $210,000
  • $210,000 − $45,000 repairs = $165,000 maximum allowable offer

In this example, $165,000 is not automatically the final offer. It is a screening number. A professional investor would still review comps, contractor bids, title issues, financing costs, holding period, resale fees, and local market risk before submitting an offer.

Why investors use the 70% Rule

The 70% Rule helps investors avoid emotional pricing. Sellers often focus on list price, comparable sales, or how much they owe. Investors need to focus on margin. The rule gives a fast way to test whether a deal is even close to workable before spending time on a full underwriting model.

It is especially useful when reviewing many leads quickly. A wholesaler, for example, may screen dozens of seller leads before identifying one worth deeper analysis. A flipper may use the rule to decide whether a property deserves a contractor walkthrough.

What the 70% Rule includes

The 30% margin between ARV and the 70% threshold is intended to cover more than profit. It often needs to absorb several cost categories:

  • Closing costs when buying and selling
  • Hard money points and interest
  • Holding costs such as taxes, insurance, utilities, and HOA dues
  • Agent commissions or resale fees
  • Unexpected repair overruns
  • Market risk during the renovation period
  • Investor profit target

70% Rule example for a house flip

Assume a property has the following numbers:

  • After-repair value: $425,000
  • Estimated repairs: $80,000
  • Target investor margin: 70%

The quick calculation is:

  • $425,000 × 70% = $297,500
  • $297,500 − $80,000 = $217,500

The investor's quick maximum allowable offer is $217,500. If the seller wants $290,000, the deal likely does not work as a flip unless the ARV is higher, the repair estimate is too conservative, or the investor accepts a much smaller profit margin.

How the 70% Rule connects to MAO

Maximum Allowable Offer, or MAO, is the broader concept behind the 70% Rule. The 70% Rule is one shortcut version of a MAO calculation. A more complete MAO formula may subtract repairs, closing costs, holding costs, resale costs, financing costs, assignment fees, and target profit separately.

That is why investors should use the 70% Rule as a first-pass filter, then confirm the offer with a full MAO calculator. If a deal barely works under the 70% Rule, it may fail once real costs are added.

When the 70% Rule works best

The rule works best when the property is a distressed single-family house, the investor plans to renovate and resell, the ARV is supported by strong comparable sales, and the repair estimate is reasonably accurate. It is less reliable when the property is already near retail condition, the market is moving quickly, the exit strategy is rental hold, or the property type is multifamily or commercial.

When the 70% Rule can fail

The biggest problem with the 70% Rule is that it looks precise even when the inputs are weak. A bad ARV or repair estimate will produce a bad offer number. If the ARV is overstated by $30,000 or the rehab budget is understated by $25,000, the investor's margin can disappear quickly.

Common failure points include:

  • Using active listings instead of sold comps
  • Ignoring bedroom, bathroom, square footage, and condition differences
  • Underestimating mechanical repairs
  • Forgetting permit costs and city requirements
  • Assuming the renovation will finish faster than it will
  • Ignoring resale commissions and seller concessions

70% Rule vs. a full deal analysis

The 70% Rule is useful because it is fast. A full deal analysis is useful because it is more accurate. Professional investors often use both. They screen quickly with the 70% Rule, then run a deeper model before making a binding offer.

A full analysis should include ARV, purchase price, repair budget, closing costs, financing, holding costs, resale costs, contingency, and profit target. For rental or BRRRR deals, it should also include rent, vacancy, operating expenses, debt service, cash flow, DSCR, and refinance assumptions.

How wholesalers use the 70% Rule

Wholesalers use the rule to estimate whether a buyer will have enough margin after paying the assignment fee. For example, if a cash buyer's likely MAO is $165,000 and the wholesaler wants a $10,000 assignment fee, the wholesaler may need to contract the property around $155,000 or less.

This is why assignment fee expectations must be grounded in buyer math. A large spread only works if the end buyer still sees a profitable deal.

How flippers use the 70% Rule

Flippers use the rule to protect profit before committing capital. Because flippers carry renovation risk, timeline risk, financing risk, and resale risk, they need enough margin to absorb surprises. The rule is most valuable before the contractor walkthrough, when the investor is deciding whether the lead deserves more time.

How BRRRR investors should adjust the rule

BRRRR investors often care less about resale profit and more about refinance value, loan proceeds, rent, and cash left in the deal. The 70% Rule can still help with acquisition discipline, but BRRRR underwriting should also include appraised value, refinance loan-to-value, monthly rent, operating expenses, debt service, and cash-on-cash return.

Investor checklist before using the 70% Rule

  • Confirm ARV using recently sold comparable properties
  • Estimate repairs by category, not only by guesswork
  • Add a contingency for unknown conditions
  • Include closing, holding, financing, and resale costs
  • Compare the result with your target profit
  • Run a full MAO calculation before submitting a serious offer

FAQ

What is the 70% Rule?

The 70% Rule says an investor should generally pay no more than 70% of a property's after-repair value minus estimated repairs when evaluating a fix-and-flip deal.

Is the 70% Rule always accurate?

No. It is a screening tool, not a full underwriting model. It depends heavily on accurate ARV and repair estimates.

What does ARV mean?

ARV means after-repair value. It is the estimated resale value of the property after renovations are completed.

Does the 70% Rule include repairs?

Yes. Repairs are subtracted after multiplying ARV by 70%.

Can wholesalers use the 70% Rule?

Yes. Wholesalers often use it to estimate buyer pricing and determine whether there is room for an assignment fee.

Should rental investors use the 70% Rule?

Rental investors can use it as a rough acquisition check, but rental analysis should focus on cash flow, cap rate, DSCR, vacancy, and operating expenses.

What is a good maximum allowable offer?

A good MAO leaves enough room for repairs, costs, risk, and profit based on your exit strategy.

What if the market is very competitive?

Some investors may use 75% or 80% of ARV in competitive markets, but that reduces the margin of safety.

How do I estimate repairs?

Start with major systems, then estimate room-by-room costs. Validate the number with contractor bids whenever possible.

What calculator should I use after the 70% Rule?

Use a MAO Calculator or Rehab Calculator to test the deal with more detailed cost assumptions.
